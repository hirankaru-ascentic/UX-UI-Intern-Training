UX Workflow Practice — Worked Examples

Discover → Synthesise → Ideate → MVP Scope, applied to three quick scenarios

1. Online learning platform — registration drop-off

Goal: 28% → 45% registration completion

Discover

  • Idea pitch: users drop off pre-signup because they haven't seen value yet
  • Proto-persona: Alex, wants proof of value before committing details
  • Competitor audit: competitors preview content before signup, we don't
  • 5 Whys: root cause — untested assumption that showing value first hurts conversion
  • AI-assisted research: hesitation spikes right after the value-prop screen
  • Empathy map: skeptical, rushed, "let me just look around first"

Mindset shift: unknown problem → understood problem

Synthesise

  • Affinity mapping: strongest theme — "no value shown before signup"
  • POV: Alex needs to see proof of value before committing details, because he doesn't trust it's worth the trade-off
  • HMW: how might we show proof of value before asking for signup, so users trust it's worth committing their details?

Mindset shift: raw insight → design challenge

Ideate

  • Crazy 8s: free preview lesson, demo video, sandbox trial, testimonials, curriculum preview, live sample class, instructor intro, progress-unlock
  • Assumption: a free preview increases perceived value enough to offset form friction
  • Shortlist: free preview lesson before signup

Mindset shift: many ideas → one bet

MVP Scope

  • Must: one free lesson, no login, signup prompt only after
  • Should: "1 of X lessons" progress indicator
  • Could: testimonial strip below preview
  • Won't: full curriculum browsing, sandbox mode, instructor intro video

Mindset shift: a bet → real-world proof

2. Online retail store — cart abandonment

Goal: 65% → 40% cart abandonment

Discover

  • Idea pitch: shipping cost surprise revealed only at final checkout step
  • Proto-persona: budget-conscious shopper, hates hidden fees
  • Competitor audit: competitors show shipping cost upfront, we don't
  • 5 Whys: root cause — fee revealed late feels like a bait-and-switch
  • AI-assisted research: abandonment spikes right after shipping cost is shown
  • Empathy map: frustrated, feels tricked, closes tab

Mindset shift: unknown problem → understood problem

Synthesise

  • Affinity mapping: strongest theme — "hidden cost surprise"
  • POV: shopper needs to know total cost early, because unexpected fees feel deceptive
  • HMW: how might we show total cost upfront for budget shoppers, so they don't feel surprised at checkout?

Mindset shift: raw insight → design challenge

Ideate

  • Crazy 8s: product-page shipping calculator, free-shipping-threshold banner, cart cost breakdown, flat-rate shipping, real-time estimator, price-match guarantee, "no surprises" badge, progress bar to free shipping
  • Assumption: showing shipping cost earlier reduces abandonment
  • Shortlist: real-time shipping estimator on the product page

Mindset shift: many ideas → one bet

MVP Scope

  • Must: zip-code shipping estimator on product page
  • Should: "free shipping over $X" banner
  • Could: cost breakdown in cart summary
  • Won't: price-match guarantee, flat-rate global shipping

Mindset shift: a bet → real-world proof

3. Fitness app — onboarding churn

Goal: 70% → 50% onboarding churn

Discover

  • Idea pitch: too many setup screens before first value moment
  • Proto-persona: busy parent, wants quick wins, low patience
  • Competitor audit: competitors reach first workout in under 2 minutes, we take 8
  • 5 Whys: root cause — onboarding front-loads all setup before any workout
  • AI-assisted research: 80% of drop-off happens at step 4 of 9 (goal-setting screen)
  • Empathy map: overwhelmed, bored, "just let me start"

Mindset shift: unknown problem → understood problem

Synthesise

  • Affinity mapping: strongest theme — "too much setup, no early win"
  • POV: busy parent needs a fast first win, because long setup feels like a barrier before value
  • HMW: how might we get new users to their first workout win in under 2 minutes, so they feel value before finishing setup?

Mindset shift: raw insight → design challenge

Ideate

  • Crazy 8s: skip-setup quick start, one-tap sample workout, setup-after-workout flow, progress teaser, gamified setup, 3-question setup, guest mode, video preview
  • Assumption: delaying full setup until after the first workout reduces churn
  • Shortlist: "try a workout first" guest mode before setup

Mindset shift: many ideas → one bet

MVP Scope

  • Must: one-tap guest workout, zero setup required
  • Should: save-progress prompt after workout completes
  • Could: gamified badge for first workout
  • Won't: full personalization engine, social sharing

Mindset shift: a bet → real-world proof

4. New product — freelancer quarterly tax estimator app

Goal: validate demand before building (greenfield, no existing product)

Discover

  • Problem interviews: how do freelancers currently estimate/pay quarterly taxes today?
  • Jobs-to-be-done: they "hire" spreadsheets, accountants, or guesswork — all feel stressful or late
  • Market scan: existing tools are accountant-facing, not freelancer-facing — gap in self-serve simplicity
  • Assumption mapping: riskiest belief — freelancers will trust an app enough to act on its tax estimate
  • Smoke test: fake-door landing page — "Get your quarterly tax number in 2 minutes" + email signup
  • 5 Whys: root cause — freelancers avoid dealing with taxes until the deadline because it feels complex and anxiety-inducing, not because tools don't exist

Mindset shift: unknown problem → understood problem

Synthesise

  • Affinity mapping: strongest theme — "tax anxiety from complexity, not lack of tools"
  • POV: freelancers need a fast, low-anxiety way to know what they owe, because current tools assume accounting knowledge they don't have
  • HMW: how might we give freelancers a trustworthy tax number in minutes, so they don't dread quarterly deadlines?

Mindset shift: raw insight → design challenge

Ideate

  • Crazy 8s: bank-linked auto-estimator, photo-of-invoice scanner, chatbot tax coach, monthly "set-aside" reminder, one-tap accountant handoff, gamified savings tracker, simple 3-question calculator, calendar-based nudges
  • Assumption: a 3-question calculator is enough to build initial trust before asking for bank access
  • Shortlist: simple 3-question tax calculator as the entry point

Mindset shift: many ideas → one bet

MVP Scope

  • Must: 3-question calculator giving a ballpark tax estimate, no signup required to see result
  • Should: email capture to save the estimate and get a deadline reminder
  • Could: shareable "set-aside" savings goal
  • Won't (this round): bank linking, full accounting integration, accountant handoff
  • Success metric: % of landing page visitors who complete the calculator and give their email — proves demand before writing real product code

Mindset shift: a bet → real-world proof

5. Client-brought problem — trustworthy after-school activities for kids

Goal: no metric given by client — team must define what "success" means before scoping anything

Discover

  • Client brief (raw): "Parents struggle to find trustworthy after-school activities for their kids" — no product, no metric, just a pain point
  • Problem interviews: parents currently rely on word-of-mouth, Facebook parent groups, and school flyers — not directories or apps
  • Jobs-to-be-done: parents "hire" a trusted friend's recommendation over any listing site, even an incomplete one
  • Market scan: existing activity directories show business-submitted info only — no signal from other real parents
  • 5 Whys: root cause — parents don't distrust the *activities*, they distrust *unverified listings with no one vouching for them*
  • Defined success metric (team-set, since none existed): % of interviewed parents who'd sign up for early access to a trust-verified finder

Mindset shift: vague complaint → testable business goal

Synthesise

  • Affinity mapping: strongest theme — "trust comes from people, not listings"
  • POV: parents need to know an activity is vetted by someone like them, because impersonal directories don't carry the same trust as word-of-mouth
  • HMW: how might we bring word-of-mouth-level trust to an online activity finder, so parents feel as confident as a personal recommendation?

Mindset shift: raw insight → design challenge

Ideate

  • Crazy 8s: parent review/rating system, "recommended by parents like you" tags, neighborhood group integration, video testimonials, verified-by-school badge, invite-only community access, local parent ambassador program, attendance-based badges
  • Assumption: reviews specifically from other verified parents (not generic star ratings) is what builds enough trust to try something new
  • Shortlist: parent review system with verified-attendance tags

Mindset shift: many ideas → one bet

MVP Scope

  • Must: attendance-verified parent reviews visible on each activity listing (e.g. "verified: 12 parents' kids attended")
  • Should: sort/filter by "most reviewed by parents near you"
  • Could: option to message a reviewing parent directly
  • Won't (this round): ambassador program, school partnership badges, in-app community groups
  • Success metric: % of early-access signups who go on to browse at least 3 verified listings — validates the trust signal before building the full platform

Mindset shift: a bet → real-world proof
